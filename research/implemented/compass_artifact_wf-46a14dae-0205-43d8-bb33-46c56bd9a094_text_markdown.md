# The AI conductor's guide to shipping real software

**A non-traditional developer — an artist, academic, and rhetorician orchestrating AI systems — can professionalize their practice by combining lightweight process frameworks, disciplined AI workflows, and a "triple-serving" strategy where every project simultaneously generates revenue, portfolio value, and academic output.** The path forward doesn't require becoming a canonical software engineer. It requires building the minimum scaffolding (roughly 50–70 hours of targeted learning) to stop getting lost, adopting a fixed-time/variable-scope shipping discipline, and leveraging the fact that a non-traditional background is an *advantage* in the emerging AI-first development landscape. The most successful people doing exactly this — Casey Reas, Lauren McCarthy, Pieter Levels, McKay Wrigley — all came from outside traditional CS and turned their outsider perspective into their defining asset.

## Process frameworks that actually work for one person

The core tension for a solo creative technologist is that most software methodologies assume teams. Scrum's daily standups, sprint planning, and retrospectives are ceremony designed for groups. What follows are the frameworks battle-tested by solo builders, ordered by relevance.

**Personal Kanban** is the simplest starting point — it has exactly two rules: visualize your work, and limit your work-in-progress. Created by Jim Benson and Tonianne DeMaria Barry, it requires nothing more than a board with columns matching your actual process (not just To-Do/Doing/Done — something like "Ideating → Prototyping → Testing → Shipped"). The WIP limit is the crucial mechanism: by capping how many things can be "in progress" simultaneously, it forces finishing before starting. For an artist-developer who habitually opens new creative threads, this single constraint is transformative. Tools: Trello (simplest), GitHub Projects (integrated with code), or a physical whiteboard.

**Shape Up**, Basecamp's methodology published free online by Ryan Singer, introduces the concept of **"appetite" over estimation** — instead of asking "how long will this take?" you ask "how much time am I willing to spend?" This inverts the usual relationship with scope. A six-week cycle (or a shortened two- or four-week variant for solo work) creates a hard boundary: ship or kill the project when time expires. The "cool-down" periods between cycles — two weeks for exploration, learning, and housekeeping — explicitly honor the creative exploration that artist-developers need. Shaping, the upfront work of defining a problem at the right level of abstraction, is itself a creative act involving sketching and visual thinking.

**PARA** (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives), Tiago Forte's organizational system, solves a different problem: the knowledge chaos that accumulates when you're simultaneously an artist, researcher, coder, and teacher. By organizing everything by actionability rather than topic — with the same four folders mirrored across Notion, your file system, and cloud storage — it gives every piece of research, code snippet, and inspiration a home. The critical insight is Forte's distinction: **"A project without a goal is a hobby. A goal without a project is a dream."** PARA doesn't tell you *how* to work, only how to organize the information around your work. Pair it with Personal Kanban for execution.

The **indie hacker shipping philosophy**, pioneered by Pieter Levels and codified in his book *MAKE*, offers something none of the above do: a radical bias toward action. Levels launched 70+ projects solo, generating roughly $250K/month in revenue with zero employees and zero VC funding. His method — ship an MVP within weeks, validate with paying customers, kill what doesn't work — treats projects like creative experiments where most will fail. His "12 Startups in 12 Months" challenge eliminates perfectionism through sheer volume. A September 2024 Hacker News discussion on solo developer workflows surfaced a critical insight from an experienced solo developer: **"It sounds more like lack of discipline than bad organization… Find a reason to be disciplined and it's amazing how many tools start to work well."**

The practical synthesis for a creative technologist: use PARA for knowledge management, Personal Kanban for daily work visualization with strict WIP limits, Shape Up's appetite concept for project scoping, and the indie hacker ethos of shipping imperfect work quickly. The minimal tool stack is Notion or Obsidian (PARA-organized knowledge base), GitHub Projects (Kanban integrated with code), and a plain-text `notes-todo` file in each project repo — append to the top, date-stamped.

## The vibe coding revolution and its real limits

The term **"vibe coding"** was coined by Andrej Karpathy in February 2025 to describe a workflow where the developer "fully gives in to the vibes, embraces exponentials, and forgets that the code even exists." Collins English Dictionary named it Word of the Year 2025. But by February 2026, Karpathy himself declared vibe coding "passé" and proposed the term **"agentic engineering"** — emphasizing that orchestrating AI agents requires genuine skill and judgment, not just acceptance of output.

Simon Willison drew the critical distinction: if an LLM wrote every line of your code but you reviewed, tested, and understood it all, that's not vibe coding — that's using an LLM. Vibe coding means building software without reviewing the AI's code. The difference matters enormously for quality and sustainability.

The emerging consensus workflow, articulated by Addy Osmani (Google Chrome engineering) and Greg Detre (who built a **60,000-line project in six weeks** without writing a single line by hand), follows a spec-first approach. Start by brainstorming requirements with AI, compile them into a `spec.md`, generate a project plan broken into bite-sized tasks, critique the plan using a different model (use Claude for planning, o3 for critique), then execute feature by feature in small loops. Detre's key mindset shift: **"Let go emotionally of the idea that you are there to write code. Your role is engineering and product manager of an AI team."**

The tool landscape has stratified by use case. **Cursor IDE** (a VS Code fork, ~$20/month) leads for iterative, agentic development — its Agent Mode writes code across multiple files, `.cursorrules` files define project-specific AI behavior, and YOLO Mode auto-runs tests to verify its own output. **Claude Code** operates in the terminal with deeper Git integration, Plan Mode for read-only analysis before writing, and the ability to spawn specialized sub-agents. For non-traditional developers who want the least friction, **Lovable** (formerly GPT Engineer, now at **$43M ARR**) and **Replit Agent** generate full-stack applications from natural-language prompts with one-click deployment. **v0 by Vercel** produces the highest-quality frontend component code from prompts or image uploads. **Bolt.new** reached roughly $40M ARR by March 2025 as the fastest path from prompt to working prototype.

The multi-model ensemble is emerging as best practice. Use Claude for conversation and coding, o3 for deep reasoning and architecture review, and Gemini 2.5 Pro for large-context analysis (dumping entire codebases into its 2M-token window). Tools like Claude Squad spawn multiple Claude Code instances working concurrently, and Cursor 2.0 supports up to **eight parallel background agents** on separate Git branches.

The failure modes are well-documented and serious. A CodeRabbit analysis from December 2025 found AI co-authored code had **1.7× more major issues** than human-written code: 75% more logic errors, 2.74× higher security vulnerabilities. GitClear's 2025 data showed an 8× increase in duplicated code blocks. The METR study from July 2025 revealed that experienced developers actually took **19% longer** using AI tools, despite *feeling* 20% faster. Google's DORA report found a 25% increase in AI usage correlated with a 7.2% *decrease* in delivery stability. The "whack-a-mole" pattern — where AI fixes one thing but breaks ten others — becomes acute once projects exceed roughly 2,000 lines. And Karpathy's own experience deploying his first vibe-coded app, MenuGen, revealed that while local prototyping was easy, deployment was "a painful slog" of environment variables, API keys, and outdated LLM knowledge.

The practical takeaway: start with high-level tools (Lovable, Replit) for rapid MVPs, graduate to Cursor for iterative development, always commit working versions to Git before asking AI to make changes, review every line of authentication and security logic, and budget for a professional developer review before putting anything into production.

## Turning the AI-conductor method into a business

The path from "person who uses AI to build things" to "person who gets paid for their AI methodology" follows a pattern visible across every successful example in the space. Every one of the major figures started by **learning in public** — sharing their workflows, mistakes, and discoveries openly — before monetizing.

Swyx (Shawn Wang), a former currency options trader who transitioned to tech, coined the "Learn in Public" philosophy that became the foundational essay of the build-in-public movement. His insight: share everything you learn, talk to yourself from three months ago, and the audience and opportunities come to you. He now runs the Latent Space newsletter and podcast (top 10 US tech), teaches a Maven cohort course on AI engineering ($500–$1,500 per student), organizes the AI Engineer Summit conference, and runs Smol AI (whose `smol-developer` open-source tool has 12,200 GitHub stars). Multiple revenue streams, all built from the same core methodology.

McKay Wrigley, a law school dropout who couldn't code 18 months before launching his first product, built **Takeoff AI** with 25,000+ paying learners by following an exact playbook: create free viral content (Twitter demos, YouTube tutorials where one Cursor video hit 456K views) → build massive audience (225K+ followers) → funnel to paid courses. His approach to productizing: the free content demonstrates competence, the paid platform provides structured learning, and open-source tools (Buildware on GitHub) establish technical credibility.

The revenue models available, ordered by speed to first dollar:

- **Productized consulting** ($2K–$15K per engagement, revenue in 2–4 weeks): package the "AI conductor" methodology as a fixed-scope service helping organizations orchestrate AI development workflows. The fractional CTO market doubled to 120,000 practitioners by 2024 at rates of $200–$500/hour. Greg Isenberg predicts "AI workflow designers will be the highest-paid consultants of the next decade."
- **Cohort-based courses** ($50–$1,500/student): Stanford Continuing Studies now offers "Vibe Coding: Building Software in Conversation with AI." Coursera has a 4-week vibe coding specialization. The market is real and growing. Ship 30 for 30 (Dickie Bush and Nicolas Cole) proved the model: start with a tweet, charge $50/person, manage in a spreadsheet and Slack, scale to 10,000+ students and a multi-million-dollar education business.
- **YouTube + newsletter funnel** (free content → paid offerings): document the methodology publicly, create viral demos of multi-AI orchestration, convert audience to courses or consulting.
- **Community/membership** (the Greg Isenberg model): paid community of practitioners sharing workflows, with his ACP framework — Audience → Community → Product — as the playbook.

Greg Isenberg's key insight frames the monetization correctly: **"No one will pay for 'AI' — they'll pay to solve a $10,000/hour problem in three clicks. Sell outcomes, hide the AI."** The "AI conductor" framing is compelling as a personal brand and methodology name, but the revenue comes from solving specific, expensive problems for specific audiences.

The non-traditional background is the asset, not the liability. Every major success story in this space — McKay (law dropout), Pieter Levels (music producer turned $250K/month solo developer), Danny Postma (web marketer who built HeadshotPro to $300K/month), Swyx (finance trader) — came from outside CS. The outsider-who-builds-with-AI narrative is exactly what audiences respond to.

## How artist-developers learn to ship

Creative technologists face a unique variant of scope creep: **exploration IS the work**. Unlike traditional software development where exploration is waste, creative coding generates genuine artistic value through discovery. The challenge isn't eliminating exploration but channeling it within constraints.

The single most effective strategy, validated across indie game development, creative coding, and solo SaaS building, is **"fixed time, variable scope"** — Shape Up's core mantra. You fix the deadline and flex what you deliver to fit. The CHAOS Report found small projects are **9× more likely to succeed** than large ones. DuPont saw productivity more than triple when adopting timeboxing in the 1980s. The mechanism works because constraints enable creativity: when you can't add more time, you must make creative decisions about what matters most.

The MoSCoW method from game development provides the scoping tool: categorize every feature as Must-have (core — without this, the project doesn't function), Should-have (important but not for v1), Could-have (nice, cut first), or Won't-have (explicitly excluded — **write these down**). The "Won't-have" list is the most important: it defines the boundaries that prevent the project from expanding into a labyrinth. Over **70% of surveyed indie game developers** cited "scope too large" as a significant factor in project failure.

The perfectionism trap has a specific psychological structure that ByteDrum's "The Art of Finishing" identifies: unfinished projects are "Schrödinger's projects — simultaneously perfect and flawed until you actually finish and put them out into the world." The fear of declaring something done opens it to criticism, so developers keep adding features to remain in the safe zone of potential. The counter is defining "done" *before starting* and refusing to expand that definition. Seth Godin frames shipping as a separate skill from creating in *Linchpin* — his seven abilities of an indispensable person explicitly list Creativity, Unique Creativity, and **Delivering** Unique Creativity as three distinct capabilities.

Creative coding communities have built shipping discipline into their culture through structured challenges. **Genuary** (genuary.art) runs every January with daily creative coding prompts using intentionally constraining parameters — "Exactly 43 lines of code," "You can only use TAU" — forcing artists to start AND finish daily. The habit of completing small things builds the muscle for completing large things. OpenProcessing hosts 100,000+ creative coders sharing sketches — the platform's architecture encourages small, shareable pieces rather than epic applications.

A developer who spent five years perfecting one app that failed, then shifted to building 30 apps in one year, reached $22K/month with the insight: **"I shifted to an indie-developer mindset: build fast, ship fast, and focus only on what's essential for the core feature."** Michael Lynch of mtlynch.io practices weekly retrospectives — "this exercise always makes me realize that I'm more productive than I feel" — and monthly retrospectives published publicly. The public accountability creates social pressure that overrides the lizard brain's resistance to shipping.

The recommended books for shipping discipline: *Shape Up* by Ryan Singer (free online — the best framework), *The Practice: Shipping Creative Work* by Seth Godin (mindset), *MAKE* by Pieter Levels (practical), and *The 12 Week Year* by Brian Moran (execution system that treats 12 weeks as a full year, creating urgency that eliminates "I have all year" complacency).

## One body of work serving three masters

The art-tech-academia triangle has been successfully navigated by a specific lineage of practitioners, and their strategies are replicable. The key insight from studying Casey Reas, Lauren McCarthy, Golan Levin, Zach Lieberman, and Daniel Shiffman is that they don't create separate work for each domain — they create **one thing that is simultaneously product, portfolio, and publication**, then document it differently for each audience.

**Research-creation**, the most developed institutional framework for this approach, is formally recognized by Canada's SSHRC (Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council) as "an approach to research that combines creative and academic research practices." Chapman and Sawchuk's foundational 2012 taxonomy identifies four forms that map directly to software development: Research FOR Creation (technical research that informs the software), Research FROM Creation (insights generated through building and deploying), Creative Presentation of Research (the software itself as a way of embodying findings), and **Creation AS Research** (the development process as a form of knowledge production, documented through reflective practice).

The "triple-serving" architecture works like this. Build software as an open-source tool — this simultaneously serves as a product (through consulting, SaaS, workshops, or commercial extensions), a portfolio piece (GitHub repos demonstrate capability, deployed tools demonstrate impact), and an academic output (software cited via Zenodo DOIs, documented in papers, presented at conferences). Then document the process as research in rhetoric/technology journals, digital humanities venues, or creative practice publications. Then present the work as art at festivals and exhibitions. Then teach with the work in courses that generate academic service credit while building community adoption.

Casey Reas exemplifies this architecture. As Professor of Design Media Arts at UCLA, he co-created Processing (open-source programming language for artists), co-founded the Processing Foundation (nonprofit), wrote *Processing: A Programming Handbook* (MIT Press — academic output and commercial product), exhibited at MoMA and the Whitney Biennial (art portfolio), and funded the Processing Foundation through NFT sales in 2021 (revenue). **His open-source software IS his art practice IS his teaching IS his research IS his commercial product.** Lauren McCarthy followed the same pattern with p5.js (10+ million users), receiving grants from Creative Capital, United States Artists, LACMA, Sundance, and Ars Electronica while holding a professorship at UCLA.

The specific publication venues for a rhetorician building software tools are remarkably well-suited. **Kairos: A Journal of Rhetoric, Technology, and Pedagogy** — the longest continuously-publishing online peer-reviewed journal in digital rhetoric since 1996 — publishes "webtexts," scholarship enacted through rhetorical use of new media. Digital Humanities Quarterly (DHQ) welcomes innovative and experimental scholarship. Leonardo (MIT Press) bridges arts, sciences, and technology. The Journal of Artistic Research (JAR) is specifically for practice-based artistic researchers. Computers and Composition serves the rhetoric-and-technology intersection.

The funding pipeline follows a clear sequence. Phase 1 (development): apply to NEH Digital Humanities Advancement Grants (up to **$350,000**) or SSHRC Insight Grants for research-creation. Phase 2 (exhibition/dissemination): Creative Capital Awards (up to **$50,000** unrestricted) or NEA Grants for Arts Projects in Media Arts ($10,000–$100,000) which explicitly support "creative code and computation" and "technology-centered creative practices." Phase 3 (sustainability): NSF funding, Chan Zuckerberg Initiative grants for essential open-source software, GitHub Sponsors. Phase 4 (publication): publish about the process in Kairos, DHQ, Leonardo, or present at the annual Digital Humanities conference, SIGGRAPH, or Computers and Writing.

The **GitHub-as-academic-portfolio** strategy has a specific technical dimension: the Zenodo-GitHub integration automatically assigns DOIs to software releases, making code citable in academic publications. GitHub Pages can host portfolio sites that integrate publications, projects, and code. The Hugo Academic template (4,800+ GitHub stars) creates academic résumé websites directly from GitHub.

## The 50-hour learning roadmap for not getting lost

The minimum viable software engineering knowledge for a creative technologist using AI tools amounts to roughly **50–70 hours of targeted learning** spread over 8–10 weeks. The single most impactful resource is **MIT's "The Missing Semester of Your CS Education"** (missing.csail.mit.edu), a free course explicitly designed to fill the gap between "knowing how to code" and "being effective with developer tools." The January 2026 edition integrates AI tools throughout, covering agentic coding, code quality, and packaging — uniquely suited for someone who already builds with AI but lacks canonical scaffolding.

**Git is week one.** You need six commands (`init`, `add`, `commit`, `push`, `pull`, `status`) and one mental model: Git takes snapshots of your project at points in time, branches are parallel timelines, and merging combines them. The most common mistake is not committing often enough — commit small, logical changes frequently with descriptive messages. AI can help resolve merge conflicts, but it cannot decide *when* to commit or *what* constitutes a logical unit of work. The best resource is Learn Git Branching (learngitbranching.js.org) — visual, interactive, no programming required. Allow 4–8 hours.

**Debugging methodology is week one, too.** The transformation is moving from "try random things" to the scientific method: reproduce the bug reliably, read the full error message (most beginners don't), form a hypothesis about the cause, test it by adding a `console.log()` or commenting out code, iterate until you find the root cause. The technique of **rubber duck debugging** — explaining the problem out loud, even to an object — forces the articulation that often reveals the answer. AI tools improved debugging problem-solving rates from 4.4% in 2023 to **69.1%** in 2025, but you still must accurately describe what's happening versus what should happen and evaluate whether AI's fix is correct. Best resource: Nicole Tietz-Sokolskaya's "A Systematic Approach to Debugging" (ntietz.com). Allow 3–5 hours.

**Project structure** (week one, 2–3 hours) is simple: every project needs a `src/` folder for code, `README.md` explaining what it does and how to run it, `.gitignore` to exclude `node_modules/` and `.env` files, and a `.env.example` with placeholder values for environment variables. The cardinal rule: **never commit secrets (API keys, passwords) to Git** — bots scan GitHub for exposed keys within minutes.

Weeks 3–6 cover **dependency management** (understand that packages are other people's code your project uses, always commit the lock file, check download counts and maintenance activity before trusting a package), **environment variables** (key-value pairs kept outside your code for secrets and configuration — the `.env` file that must never be committed), and **CI/CD basics** (on every push, automated tests run; on merge to main, code deploys automatically — GitHub Actions makes this a single YAML file). Docker is worth understanding conceptually — it packages your code, dependencies, and environment into a reproducible box — but not worth mastering until you need it.

Architecture patterns are learn-as-needed territory. The critical judgment call: **start with a monolith**. All your code in one application is perfectly fine for most projects and enormously simpler than microservices. Understand client-server architecture (frontend talks to backend), REST APIs (HTTP methods operating on resources via URLs), and monorepo versus polyrepo (for solo work, monorepo is simpler). AI tends to over-engineer architectural suggestions — you need the judgment to say "a monolith is fine here."

The companion book is *The Missing README* by Chris Riccomini and Dmitriy Ryaboy (No Starch Press, 2021) — a practical guide to everything beyond coding: working with codebases, managing dependencies, code reviews, deploying software, architecture decisions. Endorsed by engineers with 15+ years of experience who report still learning from it.

How AI changes the learning map is counterintuitive. AI makes syntax memorization and boilerplate generation less important but makes **mental models more important than ever**. You must understand what a Git branch *is*, what an environment variable *does*, and what a dependency *means* — without these models, you cannot evaluate AI output. Google's 2025 DORA Report found that "AI doesn't fix a team — it amplifies what's already there." The same applies to individuals: AI amplifies your existing understanding or amplifies your existing confusion.

## Conclusion: the conductor's advantage

The person described in this research — a multimedia artist, academic, and rhetorician orchestrating AI systems without canonical CS training — is not at a disadvantage. They are positioned at the exact intersection where the most valuable work is emerging. Every major success story in AI-first development comes from someone with domain expertise and creative vision who learned just enough engineering scaffolding to ship. The 50-hour learning roadmap eliminates the "getting lost" problem. Shape Up's appetite concept and Personal Kanban's WIP limits solve the labyrinth problem. The research-creation framework makes every shipped project simultaneously count as art, scholarship, and product. And the emerging market for AI workflow methodology — consulting, courses, communities — means the "AI conductor" identity itself is the product.

The single most important shift is from "I need to learn software engineering" to "I need to learn enough software engineering to direct AI systems effectively and ship reliably." That threshold is lower than most people think, and the creative-theoretical capacity to imagine what should exist — then orchestrate AI agents to build it — is the scarce skill in a world where code generation is increasingly commoditized.
